% ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
We present a FuXi-inspired deep learning pipeline for single-step (6-hour) global weather forecasting on a $1.5^\circ$ ERA5 grid ($240\times121$), after initial smoke experiments on a coarser $64\times32$ setup. The model ingests two consecutive timesteps and predicts the next state for 20 channels (five surface and fifteen upper-air features). The architecture combines a 3D cube embedding, a reduced-depth Swin Transformer V2 encoder for hierarchical global context, and a U-Net-style decoder for spatial reconstruction. In the current implementation, decoder upsampling uses learned transposed convolutions, while a final $1\times1$ projection plus bilinear resize is retained for exact output-grid alignment.

Training was stable overall, but skill remained sensitive to architecture depth, horizon length, and fine-tuning strategy. I present evaluation metrics (RMSE, ACC, latitude-weighted L1), qualitative comparison plots, and per-variable error analyses. I also tested full fine-tuning and PEFT/LoRA variants under multiple configurations; in the present runs, LoRA variants underperformed the strongest non-LoRA checkpoint on long-horizon metrics. Future extensions include deeper architecture sweeps at higher resolution, better PEFT targeting, stronger long-horizon regularization, and ensemble uncertainty estimation.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}

Machine learning has advanced rapidly in global weather forecasting. Models such as FourCastNet, GraphCast, Pangu-Weather, and FuXi demonstrate that learning-based systems can match or outperform classical numerical weather prediction (NWP) while running significantly faster and at lower computational cost per forecast (Pathak et al.; Lam et al.; Bi et al.; Chen et al.).

FuXi introduced a practical strategy for extending ML forecasts to longer lead times: instead of training a single model to forecast 15 days directly, FuXi trains a \emph{cascade} of specialized models that are chained autoregressively (0--5 d, 5--10 d, 10--15 d). Each model is optimized for its target window, which reduces accumulation of long-range errors and allows the use of model-specific regularization and perturbations (e.g., Perlin-noise-like initial perturbations in FuXi-style ensembles) to better capture forecast uncertainty. This cascaded approach mitigates the compounding of small biases that typically destroys forecast skill in a single monolithic model over long horizons.

This semester project focuses on implementing the \emph{core} FuXi-like module (cube embedding $\rightarrow$ Swin encoder $\rightarrow$ U-Net decoder) and understanding practical trade-offs of architecture depth, resolution, and training strategy on WeatherBench2 ERA5. The production experiments in this report use a $1.5^\circ$ global grid ($240\times121$), with early smoke/debug experiments at $64\times32$. The aim was to build a robust training pipeline, diagnose failure modes, and chart a path to a full autoregressive/cascade system in future work.

A concise description of the data flow:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Input:} Two consecutive timesteps $\rightarrow$ tensor of shape $(B, C=20, T=2, H=121, W=240)$.
  \item \textbf{Cube Embedding:} 3D convolutional blocks map local spatiotemporal cubes to latent tokens.
  \item \textbf{Swin Transformer V2 Encoder:} Multi-level, shifted-window self-attention extracts global context.
  \item \textbf{U-Net Decoder:} Hierarchical upsampling recovers spatial detail with skip connections and learned \texttt{ConvTranspose2d} upsampling blocks.
  \item \textbf{Projection \& Alignment:} A $1\times1$ convolution projects to 20 variables, and a final bilinear resize aligns the output exactly to $(121, 240)$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Implementation-Level Tensor Flow (Current AR768 Setup)}

The current production architecture in this repository uses the AR768 configuration on a $240\times121$ grid with patch size $(2,4,4)$ and Swin window size 8. The exact shape evolution is:

\[
(B,20,2,121,240)
\xrightarrow{\text{CubeEmbedding Conv3D }k=s=(2,4,4)}
(B,768,30,60)
\xrightarrow{\text{pad to }(32,64)}
(B,768,32,64)
\]

\[
\xrightarrow{\text{U-Transformer pre/down/mid/up/post}}
(B,768,32,64)
\xrightarrow{\text{crop}}
(B,768,30,60)
\xrightarrow{\text{FC + reshape}}
(B,20,120,240)
\xrightarrow{\text{bilinear align}}
(B,20,121,240)
\]

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Layer-wise tensor dimensions for the current AR768 forward path ($H\times W=121\times240$).}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Operation} & \textbf{Output shape} \\
\hline
Input & two-step history tensor & $(B,20,2,121,240)$ \\
Cube embedding & Conv3D $(k=s=(2,4,4))$ & $(B,768,30,60)$ \\
Padding & divisibility by $2\times$window ($=16$) & $(B,768,32,64)$ \\
Pre Swin stage & depth 2 at full resolution & $(B,768,32,64)$ \\
Down block & Conv2D $(k=3,s=2,p=1)$ + residual & $(B,768,16,32)$ \\
Mid Swin stage & depth 44 at half resolution & $(B,768,16,32)$ \\
Up block & skip concat + ConvTranspose2D $(k=2,s=2)$ & $(B,768,32,64)$ \\
Post Swin stage & depth 2 at full resolution & $(B,768,32,64)$ \\
Crop & remove padding & $(B,768,30,60)$ \\
Head & linear projection + reshape & $(B,20,120,240)$ \\
Alignment & bilinear resize & $(B,20,121,240)$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Convolutional operators used in the implemented FuXi blocks.}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\hline
\textbf{Module} & \textbf{Kernel} & \textbf{Stride} & \textbf{Role} \\
\hline
CubeEmbedding Conv3D & $(2,4,4)$ & $(2,4,4)$ & space-time tokenization \\
DownBlock Conv2D & $3\times3$ & $2$ & spatial downsampling \\
Residual Conv2D (inside blocks) & $3\times3$ & $1$ & local feature refinement \\
UpBlock ConvTranspose2D & $2\times2$ & $2$ & learned upsampling \\
Skip fusion Conv2D & $1\times1$ & $1$ & channel fusion after concat \\
Final projection (FC) & token-wise linear & -- & map 768 to $20\times4\times4$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{How this differs from vanilla Swin and vanilla U-Net.}
The Swin backbone here is not a hierarchical patch-merging classifier-style Swin. Instead, it uses fixed-resolution \emph{SwinStage} stacks with shifted-window blocks and SwinV2 mechanisms (scaled cosine attention + continuous relative position bias), then inserts a single U-shaped down/up bridge around the middle stack. Relative to a standard U-Net, this design keeps channel width constant and uses Swin attention blocks as the main representational engine, while convolutional residual/down/up blocks provide local inductive bias and stable spatial reconstruction.

\paragraph{Why this connection works in practice.}
Most attention depth is allocated at half resolution (44 blocks in the current AR768 setting), which lowers cost while preserving long-range context. The convolutional down/up path and skip connection re-inject local structure, and the final projection+alignment step guarantees output-grid consistency for evaluation.

\paragraph{Why these choices?}
The model uses a relatively large embedding size (512) and deep Swin blocks, which dramatically increase memory consumption. Even on A100 GPUs, a batch size above 2 caused OOM errors. Using AMP helped reduce memory usage, while a high number of dataloader workers (55) ensured efficient data streaming from disk. Warm-starting from a previous checkpoint accelerated convergence and allowed exploration of architectural changes without restarting from scratch.

\noindent\textbf{Compute:} training runs used NVIDIA A100 GPUs (mixed precision/AMP enabled) and a large dataloader worker count to keep GPUs fed. The large number of workers (e.g., 55) was required during high-throughput experiments on the cluster.

\section{Training notes and observations}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{AMP and memory}: Mixed precision significantly reduced memory footprint and accelerated training, allowing larger embeddings and batch throughput.
  \item \textbf{Checkpointing}: Training resumed from the foundation checkpoint above to continue experiments; this allowed rapid iteration on architecture and loss choices.
  \item \textbf{Batch size and gradients}: Small batch sizes (2) were used due to memory constraints at this configuration; gradient noise was mitigated by appropriate learning-rate scheduling and gradient accumulation when necessary.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Losses and Metrics}

We used standard metrics and latitude weighting as described below.

\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{lat-L1}}=\frac{\sum_i \cos\phi_i\,|y_i-\hat{y}_i|}{\sum_i \cos\phi_i}
\]

\[
\mathrm{RMSE}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_i (y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2}
\]

\[
\mathrm{ACC}=\frac{\sum_i (y_i-\bar y)(\hat{y}_i-\overline{\hat{y}})}
{\sqrt{\sum_i (y_i-\bar y)^2}\sqrt{\sum_i (\hat{y}_i-\overline{\hat{y}})^2}}
\]

\chapter{Challenges: Depth vs. Resolution Tradeoff}


\textbf{CODE:}\\
\url{https://github.com/Artamta/Fuxi-Updated-V2}\\[1.6cm]

One of the central technical observations of this work is the tradeoff between architecture depth and data resolution. At low spatial resolution (e.g., $64\times32$), deep transformer stacks (many Swin layers) can fail to learn meaningful gradients and may produce near-constant outputs -- a symptom sometimes referred to as \emph{field collapse}. We observed this empirically:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Loss of local gradients:} Downsampling strongly attenuates the small- and medium-scale features that provide the model with local gradients to learn. Attention layers operating on coarse tokens therefore receive weak, low-variance signals.
  \item \textbf{Difficulty for deep attention stacks:} Deeper Swin stacks increase representational capacity but also require informative gradients across layers. On low-resolution inputs, the gradients can vanish or fail to propagate structured spatial information, causing the network to converge to a trivial, low-variance solution.
  \item \textbf{Practical mitigation:} We found better behavior when (a) keeping the Swin depth modest, (b) complementing attention with convolutional residual blocks that preserve local structure, and (c) carefully tuning learning rates, batch sizes, and normalization. Mixed-precision training and checkpoint warm-starts also helped stabilize deeper experiments.
\end{itemize}

This tradeoff motivates the need for higher-resolution training data ($1^\circ$ or $0.25^\circ$) or architectural modifications (stronger convolutional inductive biases, multi-scale positional encodings) to allow deeper transformers to be effectively trained.

\chapter{Future Work: Cascade, PEFT, and Architecture Refinement}

Building on FuXi's insight, a practical route to multi-day forecasting is an autoregressive cascade of specialized models. Each model focuses on a particular lead-time window (short, medium, long), which helps prevent a single model's errors from compounding uncontrolled across very long horizons.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{cascade.png}
    \caption{FuXi-style autoregressive cascade (short/medium/long). The cascade trains specialized models for successive windows and chains them at inference time. Perlin-noise-style perturbations can be applied to generate ensemble members and stabilize training.}
    \label{fig:cascade}
\end{figure}

The current checkpoint comparison runs indicate that PEFT/LoRA requires further work in this setup. For example, at 15-day summary horizon, the non-LoRA emb768 checkpoint achieves mean RMSE/ACC of 161.39/0.408, while representative LoRA runs are weaker (e.g., rank-32 medium: 228.42/0.394; rank-16 medium: 249.87/0.345; rank-8 short: 366.49/0.280).

\begin{table}[!htbp]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\hline
Run & Mean RMSE (15d) & Mean ACC (15d) \\
\hline
Emb768 baseline (non-LoRA) & 161.39 & 0.408 \\
LoRA rank-32 (medium) & 228.42 & 0.394 \\
LoRA rank-16 (medium) & 249.87 & 0.345 \\
LoRA rank-8 (short) & 366.49 & 0.280 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\caption{Observed 15-day rollout summary from current checkpoint comparison runs (lower RMSE and higher ACC are better).}
\label{tab:peft_gap}
\end{table}

Planned extensions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Train three models specialized for 0--5 d, 5--10 d, and 10--15 d; chain them autoregressively at inference time.
  \item Run a structured PEFT ablation: stage-specific LoRA rank/alpha, expanded target modules, LoRA+partial-base updates, and scheduler tuning to close the current LoRA-vs-full-finetune gap.
  \item Finalize reconstruction design by replacing the remaining output-head bilinear alignment with a fully learned upsampling/projection path while preserving exact $121\times240$ output shape.
  \item Perform controlled Swin/U-Net architecture ablations (pre/mid/post depth split, window size, skip-projection design, and transposed-convolution settings) at $1.5^\circ$ and higher resolutions.
  \item Use small stochastic perturbations (e.g., Perlin-noise-like spatial perturbations) on initial conditions to generate ensembles and reduce sensitivity to deterministic biases.
  \item Scale training to $1^\circ$ and $0.25^\circ$ grids with careful warm-starting from the foundation model and memory-efficient training strategies.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Practical Lessons and Final Remarks}

This project provided concrete, hands-on lessons:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Pipeline engineering:} Building the cube embedding $\rightarrow$ Swin $\rightarrow$ U-Net pipeline reveals many practical pitfalls (shape mismatches, padding, window alignment) that are invisible in simple CV models.
  \item \textbf{Hyperparameter sensitivity:} Learning rate, batch size, and normalization choices have outsized effect on stability at low resolution.
  \item \textbf{Architecture choice:} Attention + convolution hybrids are currently the most robust design for global weather tasks, but they must be sized to match data resolution.
  \item \textbf{Compute considerations:} Training at high resolution and depth requires substantial compute (A100 or similar), careful checkpointing, and extensive tuning.
\end{itemize}

\section{Explicit Time and Compute Limitations}

To make the submission boundaries explicit, the following constraints directly shaped the final scope:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{GPU-memory ceiling:} Deep Swin stacks at high spatial resolution forced conservative batch sizes and limited the number of full ablation runs that could be completed within one semester.
  \item \textbf{Wall-time budget:} Multi-day autoregressive evaluations (especially 60--90 day diagnostics) are expensive, so only a focused set of checkpoint comparisons could be audited end-to-end in this submission cycle.
  \item \textbf{PEFT search budget:} LoRA stage/rank/target-module sweeps were explored, but the full combinatorial PEFT tuning space remains incomplete because each candidate requires both retraining and long-horizon evaluation.
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility priority over breadth:} Compute was allocated preferentially to stable, reproducible pipelines and verified figures rather than maximizing the number of partially validated experimental branches.
\end{itemize}
